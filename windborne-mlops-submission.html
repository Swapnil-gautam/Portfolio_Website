Describe the most complex ML model inference you have run. Include details on the compute stack you used and any optimizations you made.

The most complex inference setup I have run was an edge computer vision system for manufacturing monitoring. It ran both YOLO models for machine state detection and PaddleOCR for CNC HMI text extraction across multiple camera streams. The difficult part was not just the models, but running multiple cameras concurrently on the same edge device while keeping latency low enough for real-time monitoring.

The compute stack used quantized models with TensorRT acceleration, plus a multi-threaded inference pipeline so camera capture, model inference, OCR, and post-processing could run simultaneously without blocking each other. I spent most of the optimization work finding the right balance between latency and accuracy: testing model sizes, quantization settings, OCR confidence thresholds, and frame sampling rates until the system was fast enough for production while still reliable under factory lighting, glare, and camera angle changes.

What's the most pain you've faced running production systems? What's an interesting change you made to increase resilience or uptime?

One of the most painful production issues I faced was in Wizio AI Physio Coach, where the standard human pose keypoint detection method was not accurate enough for very small physiotherapy motions. The system could work well for larger movements, but for small joint angle changes the keypoints were noisy enough that the downstream exercise counting and form correction became unreliable.

The interesting change we made was to train our own pose/keypoint model for the movements and camera conditions we cared about, then integrate it back into the existing inference pipeline. That improved the quality of the keypoints without requiring us to redesign the whole product. It also made the system more resilient because the downstream logic was no longer trying to compensate for unstable inputs from a generic pose model.
