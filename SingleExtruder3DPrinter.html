3D Printer & Filament Extruder

A connected mechatronics project: a custom single-extruder FDM 3D printer paired with a desktop filament extruder, covering the full path from raw plastic to printed part.

CAD Fusion 360 Arduino Marlin Stepper Motors Thermal Control

Problem Statement

Commercial 3D printers hide a lot of the mechanical and control tradeoffs behind polished hardware, and they always produce failed prints and offcuts that become plastic waste. Building both a printer and a filament extruder together forces you to understand the full FDM pipeline — melting and feeding plastic, controlled extrusion, coordinated motion — and closes the loop by turning waste back into usable filament.

Approach

Both systems were designed as practical mechatronics builds: rigid mechanical foundation first, then controlled motion or feed, then heating and stability tuning. Each subsystem was assembled and tested independently before full integration.

Solution

Single Extruder 3D Printer

  • Designed and assembled the frame, carriage, bed, and extruder mount.
  • Integrated stepper motors, end stops, heated bed, hot end, and controller electronics.
  • Configured Marlin firmware parameters for axis steps, acceleration, jerk, thermal limits, and homing.
  • Calibrated extrusion flow, bed leveling, first-layer adhesion, and print temperature profiles.

Filament Extruder

  • Built the extrusion body, hopper, nozzle, and drive mechanism for plastic feedstock.
  • Integrated heating elements and temperature sensing for controlled melting.
  • Used motor speed control to tune the feed and pull rate.
  • Iterated on cooling distance and winding behavior to improve filament diameter consistency.
Pairing the two projects connected mechanical design, embedded control, and feedback tuning into one workflow — useful groundwork for later robotics builds.

Challenges

On the printer, mechanical tolerances were the biggest learning curve — small frame misalignments, belt tension changes, or bed-leveling errors showed up directly in print quality, so the build required iterative measurement and test prints. On the extruder, filament diameter consistency was the recurring issue: temperature drift, inconsistent feedstock, and cooling-rate changes all affected output, so the prototype required repeated tuning across heater settings and pull speeds.
